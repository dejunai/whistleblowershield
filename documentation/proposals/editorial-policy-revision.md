<p>WhistleblowerShield.org is maintained as a nonpartisan educational reference. Our goal is to present legal information in a neutral, citation-forward format <em>(meaning every legal claim links directly to its official source)</em> that helps the public understand whistleblower protections and official reporting frameworks in the United States.</p>
<p>We hold ourselves to two standards simultaneously: legal accuracy, and genuine usefulness to someone who is not a lawyer.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Who Maintains This Site</h2>
<p>WhistleblowerShield.org is currently maintained by an independent amateur legal researcher and developer. The site is designed to grow — the editorial contact address and role structure are intended to support a contributing team as the project expands. Contributors are not licensed attorneys unless specifically identified as such on the page where their review is noted.</p>
<p>We are not a law firm, a legal services provider, or a government agency. We operate as a public-interest educational project. Our editorial independence is not subject to donor influence, commercial relationships, or political affiliation.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Sourcing Standards</h2>
<p>We prioritize sources in this order of authority:</p>
<ol>
<li><strong>Official legal texts</strong> — statutes, regulations, and official legislative repositories.</li>
<li><strong>Official government resources</strong> — agency pages, <span class="ws-term-highlight" data-tooltip="Independent oversight offices within federal and state agencies responsible for investigating waste, fraud, and abuse. They operate separately from agency leadership to ensure impartiality.">inspectors general</span>, and official guidance.</li>
<li><strong>Court opinions and official case materials</strong> — included selectively where directly relevant.</li>
<li><strong>Reputable nonpartisan organizations and legal associations</strong> — used for secondary context only.</li>
</ol>
<p>Whenever practical, we provide citations and direct links to primary official sources. Paraphrased or summarized content is always attributed.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Nonpartisan and Neutral Language Standards</h2>
<p>We do not publish political commentary or partisan messaging.</p>
<p>Our content describes what the law does — not what it means politically. We use neutral, descriptive language: <em>provides, prohibits, defines, requires, permits, establishes.</em></p>
<p>We do not characterize legal outcomes as political victories or defeats, describe officials or agencies as allies or opponents, or use persuasion-driven or opinion-driven framing. We do not describe protections as "strong" or "weak" — these are comparative judgments that are difficult to apply consistently and can read as editorial opinion rather than factual description. Where the law is ambiguous or subject to differing court interpretations, we note the ambiguity rather than attempt to resolve it editorially.</p>
<p><strong>On describing the scope of protections:</strong> Our summaries describe what a jurisdiction's law specifically does — who it covers, what disclosures it protects, what remedies it provides, and what it does not cover. We state these facts directly and concretely, because users deciding whether they are protected deserve clear answers. For example: noting that a statute covers independent contractors as well as employees, or that a law applies only to public sector workers, or that a filing deadline is 180 days rather than one year — these are the factual details that matter most to our users. We describe what the law does. We do not editorialize about whether it is good policy or whether its protections are sufficient.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Writing Standards</h2>
<p>All public-facing content is written to be readable by someone without legal training. We target a 9th to 10th grade reading level for summary content. Legal terms are explained on first use. Jurisdiction summaries are structured to answer the most important question first: does this jurisdiction protect whistleblowers, and what does that mean in practice?</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">AI Content Policy</h2>
<p>Artificial intelligence tools may be used in research, drafting, and development. AI is used as a technical collaborator — for structural organization, code generation, and research assistance. AI-generated content is not published as final content on this site. All published legal content is human-authored and reviewed before publication. All legal data points and primary citations are selected and verified by human editors. Any errors on WhistleblowerShield.org are human-made errors.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Update Practice and Last Reviewed</h2>
<p>Jurisdiction pages display a <strong>Last Reviewed</strong> date reflecting the most recent substantive review of that page's content. Notable legal changes are documented in the <a href="/legal-updates/">Legal Updates</a> log with a description of what changed and when.</p>
<p>Formatting corrections, broken link repairs, and typographical fixes may be made without a log entry. Material changes to legal content are always logged.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Scope</h2>
<p>This site focuses on educational reference content covering whistleblower protections in U.S. jurisdictions. We do not provide individualized legal guidance, strategic recommendations, or instructions designed to circumvent legal restrictions. For individual guidance, see the <a href="/get-help/">Get Help</a> directory.</p>

<h2 class="wp-block-heading">Conflicts of Interest and Donor Influence</h2>
<p>Donations support site operations and maintenance only. Donors do not control or influence editorial decisions. WhistleblowerShield.org has no affiliate relationships and receives no compensation for directory listings or external links. Where a potential conflict of interest could reasonably be perceived, we aim to disclose it or avoid the conflict entirely.</p>
