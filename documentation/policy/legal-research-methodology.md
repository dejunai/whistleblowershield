# Legal Research Methodology

## Purpose

This document describes how legal research is conducted for
WhistleblowerShield and establishes the standards a contributor
must meet before content is entered into the platform.

It is written for lay contributors — people without formal
legal training who are willing to do careful, honest research.
It is also an honest account of the platform's current
methodology and its limitations.

---

## Who This Is Written For

WhistleblowerShield is built by an amateur legal researcher,
not a licensed attorney. Contributors are not assumed to have
legal training. What is assumed is:

- a genuine commitment to accuracy
- willingness to verify claims against primary sources
- honesty about what you do and don't understand
- caution about publishing anything you cannot verify

If you are uncertain about a legal interpretation, the right
answer is to say so in a review note — not to guess, and not
to publish content you cannot stand behind.

---

## The Role of AI in Research

AI tools are used actively in the research and drafting process.
This is not hidden — it is disclosed in the Editorial Policy
and documented here.

AI is used for:

- generating an initial summary of whistleblower protections
  in a jurisdiction, drawing on its training knowledge
- explaining legal concepts, terms, or statutes in plain
  English when the primary source text is difficult to parse
- identifying candidate statutes, cases, and agencies to
  research further
- drafting summary content for human review and revision

AI is not used as a primary legal source. AI-generated
summaries contain errors, hallucinations, and outdated
information. Every factual claim produced by AI must be
independently verified against a primary source before
it can be entered into the platform.

The working principle is: **AI drafts, humans verify.**

Any errors on WhistleblowerShield.org are human-made errors —
meaning a human reviewed and approved the content before
it was published. This is not a disclaimer to hide behind.
It is a reminder that the human review step is where accuracy
is either established or failed.

## Step-by-Step Research Process

### Step 1 — Generate an Initial Summary

Use an AI tool to generate a comprehensive summary of
whistleblower protections in the target jurisdiction.

Ask for:
- the primary whistleblower protection statute(s)
- who is covered (employees, contractors, public sector, etc.)
- what disclosures are protected
- what retaliation is prohibited
- how to report and to which agency
- key filing deadlines
- notable limitations or exclusions

Treat this output as a research starting point, not a draft
ready for publication. It will contain errors.

---

### Step 2 — Identify and Locate Primary Sources

From the AI summary, extract the specific statutes, regulations,
and agencies named. Then locate each one at its official source.

Preferred primary sources:

**Federal:**
- govinfo.gov — United States Code and Code of Federal Regulations
- congress.gov — federal legislation and bill history
- federalregister.gov — agency rulemaking
- Official agency websites (SEC, OSHA, IRS, OSC, etc.)

**State:**
- Official state legislature websites (e.g., leginfo.legislature.ca.gov
  for California, capitol.texas.gov for Texas)
- State agency official pages

If the AI has cited a statute, find the actual statute text.
Read the relevant section. Confirm that what the AI said the
statute does is what the statute text actually says.

If you cannot find a cited statute at an official source,
do not use it. Flag it as unverified in your research notes.

---

### Step 3 — Verify Each Factual Claim

For each factual claim in the AI summary, ask:

- Can I find this in the statute or regulation text itself?
- Does the official source say what the AI said it says?
- Is the citation format correct? (statute name, title, section)
- Is the official source link active and pointing to the
  right document?

Common AI errors to watch for:

- Citing statutes that don't exist or have the wrong section number
- Describing coverage more broadly than the statute actually provides
- Confusing federal and state law protections
- Citing outdated versions of statutes that have been amended
- Overstating financial award amounts or eligibility criteria

When you find a discrepancy between the AI summary and the
primary source, the primary source is correct. Revise accordingly.

---

### Step 4 — Clarify What You Don't Understand

Legal text is often difficult to parse. When you encounter
statutory language you don't fully understand, ask an AI
to explain it in plain English — then verify that explanation
makes sense against the statute text.

Example: if a statute uses the phrase "reasonable cause to
believe," ask what that standard means in practice and how
courts have typically interpreted it. Then check whether the
explanation aligns with how the statute is discussed in
official agency guidance or court decisions.

This is not a substitute for legal expertise. It is a tool
for making primary sources comprehensible to a lay researcher.
Use it freely, but always return to the primary source to
confirm your understanding.

---

### Step 5 — Research Significant Case Law

Once the statutory framework is verified, identify three
significant cases where court interpretation set or changed
the legal precedent in that jurisdiction.

Ask an AI to identify candidate cases, then verify each case:

- Confirm the case name, citation, court, and year
- Confirm the case exists and the citation is accurate
  (Google Scholar, Justia, or official court websites)
- Confirm the holding as described matches what the court
  actually decided
- Confirm the case is relevant to whistleblower protection
  specifically, not just employment law generally

Three cases is a practical minimum for demonstrating how
the law has been interpreted in practice. More can be included
if they are directly material to understanding the jurisdiction's
protections.

Do not include a case you cannot verify independently.
A fabricated or incorrectly cited case is worse than
no case citation at all.

---

### Step 6 — Assemble and Review

With verified statutes and cases in hand, review the AI-generated
summary against your findings and revise it to reflect what
the primary sources actually say.

Before moving to data entry, confirm:

- Every statute cited exists and the citation is correct
- Every official source URL is active and points to the
  right document
- The summary accurately describes who is covered, what is
  protected, and how to seek relief
- Case citations are accurate and holdings are correctly described
- You can explain, in plain English, what the summary says —
  if you cannot explain it, it is not ready to publish

If any part of the summary contains a claim you could not
verify, remove it or flag it explicitly as unverified.
Unverified content must not be published.

## Source Reliability Tiers

When evaluating sources, apply the four-tier framework
defined in source-verification-policy.md:

**Tier 1 — Primary legal sources** (required for all factual claims)
Statutes, regulations, court decisions, official legislative
repositories.

**Tier 2 — Government guidance**
Agency publications, inspector general reports, official
program documentation. Useful for understanding how statutes
are applied in practice.

**Tier 3 — Academic and legal analysis**
Law review articles, legal textbooks. Useful for context
and interpretation, but must not replace primary citations.

**Tier 4 — Advocacy and commentary**
Nonprofit publications, legal commentary. May provide useful
summaries but should not be treated as authoritative.

Blogs, anonymous commentary, marketing content, and
unverified online summaries are not acceptable sources
under any circumstances.

---

## What to Do When You're Unsure

If you are unsure whether a legal claim is accurate:

- Do not publish it
- Flag it in the review status field as Needs Review
- Note specifically what you could not verify
- A licensed attorney review is the appropriate next step
  for content you cannot resolve as a lay researcher

Publishing uncertain content with a "Pending Review" badge
is acceptable. Publishing uncertain content without flagging
it is not.

The platform's credibility depends on the honesty of its
review process. A "Pending Human Review" badge displayed
publicly is not a failure — it is an accurate representation
of the content's status. That honesty is what distinguishes
this platform from resources that obscure their limitations.

---

## Limitations of This Methodology

This methodology produces research that is:

- Grounded in primary sources
- Verified by a careful lay researcher
- Honest about its review status

It is not:

- Reviewed by a licensed attorney (unless specifically noted)
- Guaranteed to be current at the time of your visit
- Comprehensive — gaps in coverage do not mean gaps in the law

These limitations are disclosed publicly on every jurisdiction
page through the disclaimer notice, review status badges,
and last-reviewed dates.

The goal is not perfection. It is honest, careful, sourced
research that is transparent about what it is and what it is not.
