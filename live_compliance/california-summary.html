How the Law Works

The primary whistleblower protection in California is codified in California Labor Code Section § 1102.5. This statute prohibits employers from engaging in retaliation against employees who disclose information to a government or law enforcement agency, to a person with authority over the employee, or to another employee who has the authority to investigate, discover, or correct the violation.

  • Scope: Covers public and private sector employees, individuals acting on behalf of the employer, and independent contractors performing services pursuant to a contract.

Protected Disclosures

In the jurisdiction of California, a disclosure is protected if the employee has reasonable cause to believe the information evidences:

  • A violation of a state or federal statute (such as laws against discrimination, wage theft, fraud, or environmental violations).
  • A violation of or noncompliance with a local, state, or federal rule or regulation.
  • Unsafe working conditions or practices at the employee's workplace or site of service.

Reporting and Remedies

1. Making a Protected Disclosure

To qualify for protection under § 1102.5, a whistleblower must generally disclose information to a person with authority, a government agency, or a law enforcement body. For general misconduct where the specific agency is unknown, the California State Attorney General's Whistleblower Hotline (1-800-952-5225) serves as a centralized referral service to the appropriate investigative body.

2. Seeking Relief from Retaliation

If an employer punishes you for making a disclosure, you may take the following steps to invoke your legal protections:

  1. Administrative Filing: You may file a formal retaliation complaint with the California Labor Commissioner's Office (Division of Labor Standards Enforcement, DLSE) within the statute of limitations (generally one year from the date of the retaliatory act).
  2. Civil Litigation: Alternatively, an employee may file a private lawsuit in Superior Court to seek damages, such as back pay, reinstatement, or compensatory damages (money to cover your actual losses).

Case Law and Outcomes

Standard for Proving Retaliation

In Lawson v. PPG Architectural Finishes, Inc. (2022), the California Supreme Court ruled that whistleblowers need only prove that retaliation was a contributing factor in an adverse employment action.1

Protection for Internal Reporting

California courts have clarified that reporting a violation to a supervisor or internal manager carries the same legal weight and protection as reporting to an outside government agency.2

The "First Reporter" Rule

In Hager v. County of Los Angeles (2014), the court affirmed that an employee does not lose protection simply because they were not the very first person to disclose the specific wrongdoing. Protecting "subsequent" whistleblowers is vital to ensuring systemic issues are corrected.3

Limitations and Ramifications

Unprotected Acts: Protections generally do not extend to the disclosure of personal grievances or disagreements regarding lawful policy decisions that do not involve a violation of law or regulation.

Media Reporting: Direct reporting to news media is generally not a protected disclosure under California's primary whistleblower statutes. To secure legal protection, report to a government agency or person with authority first.

Malicious Claims: California law does not protect disclosures that are known to be false or made with reckless disregard for the truth (including reports made with the intent to cause harm rather than expose wrongdoing). Employers may have grounds for legal recourse if a report is proven to be intentionally malicious.
