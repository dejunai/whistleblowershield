How We Use AI

AI is not a shortcut here. It is a deliberate architectural choice made with the end user in mind: a worker, potentially in crisis, who needs accurate information they can act on.

This page documents how AI is used, why each tool was chosen, and what the human layer looks like. It is written for developers and researchers, not for people seeking legal guidance.

What AI Does Not Do

Before the toolchain: the constraints matter more than the capabilities.

  • AI does not publish anything. Every record passes through human review before it enters the database or appears on the site.
  • AI does not determine whether a statute applies to a specific situation. The site is a reference, not legal advice. That distinction is architectural, not a disclaimer.
  • AI does not resolve taxonomy proposals. When a model identifies a legal concept that doesn't fit an existing category, it flags it. A human decides whether the category should be added, modified, or left as-is.
  • AI does not verify that a cited case is still good law. That is a human reviewer responsibility flagged explicitly in the review queue.

The Toolchain

Each tool has a defined lane. No single model is trusted to do everything.

Claude (Anthropic) — Architecture and reasoning

Used for system design, architectural decisions, and keeping the end user visible throughout the build. The reasoning-partner quality produces something that pushes back when a design decision drifts from the mission, surfaces downstream consequences of upstream choices, and maintains awareness of the person the site is built to serve.

Claude Code — Large-scale implementation

Used for mechanical execution: codebase-wide audits, global find-and-replace passes, file generation at scale. Fast and thorough for tasks where completeness matters more than judgment. Implements decisions made in the reasoning layer — it does not replace it.

Perplexity — Uninformed audit

Used specifically because it has no prior context on the project. Fresh eyes on code files catch drift, inconsistency, and documentation rot that familiarity obscures. The absence of architectural knowledge is the feature, not a limitation.

Gemini, ChatGPT, Grok — First-pass legal research

Used to generate structured data for the 57-jurisdiction legal dataset. Each model produces a batch; a human reviews the output before anything reaches the database. These models are positioned as first-pass researchers, not authorities. The research prompt encodes this directly:

A record with five confident, sourced fields is more valuable than a record with fifteen fields where three are invented. Honest gaps do not cause harm. Confidently wrong records do.

The Data Pipeline

Legal data enters the system through a structured JSON pipeline. Every batch produced by an AI model carries:

  • A meta block identifying the model, version, jurisdiction, and generation date.
  • An integrity block — the model's honest self-report on what it could not confirm, which citations it omitted because sources could not be verified, and whether the batch was truncated for quality reasons.
  • A completion sentinel that the ingest tool checks before processing — a missing or empty sentinel aborts ingest entirely, preventing partial batches from silently producing incomplete records.

The integrity block is not a formality. Models are explicitly instructed that honest error reporting is more valuable than clean-looking output. An unreported error that passes through ingest is far more costly than one that surfaces in the review queue.

All records enter a human review queue before publication. The AI produces the draft. A human verifies it.

Source Discipline

The research prompt enforces a strict approved-source hierarchy.

Statute sources, in priority order:

  1. Official legislature URL for the jurisdiction
  2. uscode.house.gov / congress.gov for federal statutes
  3. legiscan.com — acceptable secondary source
  4. law.justia.com — acceptable secondary source

Case law sources, in priority order:

  1. Official court websites
  2. courtlistener.com — PACER-sourced
  3. casetext.com
  4. law.justia.com

Models attempt sources in order, use the first that yields a trustworthy result, and stop. scholar.google.com is explicitly rejected — URLs are unstable and canonical identity cannot be confirmed. If no approved source yields a verifiable URL, the field is omitted. A missing URL is correct output. A fabricated URL is not.

Taxonomy Discipline

All legal data is classified against a controlled vocabulary covering disclosure types, protected classes, disclosure targets, adverse action types, process types, remedies, and fee shifting. Models may only use registered term slugs in record arrays.

When a legal concept does not fit an existing category, models are instructed to propose a new term rather than approximate with an existing one or invent a slug and insert it directly. Proposals surface in the batch output and are resolved by a human before any term is added to the vocabulary. The taxonomy grows from evidence, not assumption.
