## What this batch tells you strategically

**The federal statute forced precision from both models** in a way the California statutes did not. When the law is clearly written with explicit standards, explicit deadlines, and Supreme Court decisions on point, both models produce cleaner outputs. This is useful signal: your human review burden will likely be heavier on state statutes with ambiguous SOLs and sparse case law than on major federal statutes.

**The proposal pipeline is working.** One run after you added the family-member example, Grok produced a correctly formed proposal on a different concept entirely. The example did its job. The next question is whether ChatGPT will start proposing in future runs, or whether it will consistently prefer `has-details` workarounds. That behavioral difference between the two models is worth watching.

**`exhaustionrequired` needs clearer schema guidance.** The Grok/ChatGPT split on this field (`false` vs `true` with `exhaustiondetails`) is a real legal distinction that the schema does not currently guide well. Worth adding a schema note: "exhaustionrequired should be true whenever the statute requires a mandatory administrative filing step before a civil action is permitted, even if the pathway to court is available after a waiting period."
