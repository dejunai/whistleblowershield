Wyoming Whistleblower Statutory Research & Findings
Based on web research and the application of strict statutory verification rules, Wyoming operates with extremely limited statutory whistleblower protections, relying heavily instead on common law precedent.
**How many Wyoming Whistleblower statutes can reasonably be found?**Exactly three (3) verifiable state-level statutory protections exist. Wyoming lacks any broad private-sector whistleblower protection act. The three enforceable statutes are:
Wyo. Stat. § 9-11-103: State Employees and Officials Whistle Blower Act (protects only state employees).
Wyo. Stat. § 27-11-109(e): Occupational Health and Safety Act Anti-Retaliation (protects employees reporting workplace safety hazards).
Wyo. Stat. § 42-4-304: Wyoming Medicaid False Claims Act Anti-Retaliation (protects employees reporting Medicaid fraud).

Wyoming Common Law Coverage: Wrongful Discharge in Violation of Public Policy
Because Wyoming is an "at-will" employment state without a general private-sector whistleblower statute, private-sector workers must rely on the common law tort of Wrongful Discharge in Violation of Public Policy.
How Coverage Works
Under Wyoming common law (established in key state Supreme Court cases like Allen v. Safeway Stores Inc., 699 P.2d 277 (Wyo. 1985) and refined in McLean v. Hyland Enterprises, Inc., 34 P.3d 1262 (Wyo. 2001)), an employer cannot fire an at-will employee if the termination violates a well-established public policy.
To succeed, an employee must prove:
The discharge violates a fundamental public policy clearly articulated in the state constitution, a statute, or administrative regulations.
The employee was discharged because of their protected conduct (e.g., refusing to commit an illegal act, or reporting an illegality).
The Preclusion Rule: Crucially, Wyoming courts rule that this common law tort is unavailable if there is already a statutory remedy available to the employee. For example, if an employee is fired for reporting an OSHA violation, they must use the OSHA statute (§ 27-11-109); they cannot sue under the common law public policy exception.
