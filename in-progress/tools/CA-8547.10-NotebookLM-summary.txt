California Government Code § 8547.10 establishes the whistleblower retaliation protections and remedies specifically for **University of California (UC) employees, officers, faculty members, and applicants**. 

Under this statute, individuals are protected against actual or attempted reprisals, threats, coercion, and adverse personnel actions—such as termination, demotion, suspension, or unfavorable evaluations—resulting from their protected disclosures. Protected disclosures cover reports of **government misconduct, corruption, abuse of authority, waste of funds, or public health concerns**. To prove retaliation, the employee must demonstrate by a preponderance of the evidence that their protected activity was a "contributing factor" to the adverse action, which then shifts the burden to the university to prove it had a legitimate, non-retaliatory reason for its actions.

A defining feature of § 8547.10 is its **strict administrative exhaustion requirement**. Before an employee can file a civil lawsuit to seek compensatory damages, punitive damages, and attorney's fees, they must first file an internal complaint with a supervisor, manager, or an officer designated by the UC Regents. Court access is only preserved if the university fails to reach a decision within the time limits set by the Regents, or if the complaint is not satisfactorily addressed within a maximum safety-valve period of **18 months**. Complaints must also be filed within a strict **12-month statute of limitations** stemming from the most recent act of reprisal.

**The Larger Context of California University Systems**

When compared to other higher education systems in California, the UC whistleblower framework is nearly identical to the state university system but diverges significantly from the community college model.

*   **The California State University (CSU) System (§ 8547.12):**
    The CSU whistleblower statute serves as a direct analogue to the UC model. It protects CSU faculty, officers, and applicants using the exact same structural mechanisms. Like UC employees, CSU staff face a **12-month statute of limitations** and are subject to the same **internal exhaustion requirement**; they must file an internal complaint with a designated CSU officer or supervisor, retaining the right to a civil lawsuit only if the trustees fail to resolve the issue within established timeframes or the 18-month window. Both systems utilize the same "contributing-factor" burden-shifting framework and offer identical remedies, including compensatory and punitive damages.
*   **The Community College System (§ 87160):**
    Governed by the Reporting by Community College Employees of Improper Governmental Activities Act, community college protections share the same 12-month statute of limitations and types of protected disclosures as the UC and CSU systems, but differ heavily in process and remedies. 
    *   **No Exhaustion Required:** Unlike UC and CSU personnel, community college employees are **not required to exhaust internal administrative remedies**.
    *   **Different Enforcement & Remedies:** Rather than focusing on civil lawsuits with compensatory and punitive damages, community college cases are handled by the State Personnel Board or law enforcement. The available remedies focus on making the employee whole administratively, providing for **reinstatement, back pay, restoration of lost service credit, and expungement of adverse records** rather than punitive payouts.
    *   **Burden of Proof:** The community college statute notably lacks the specific contributing-factor burden-shifting framework outlined in the UC and CSU models.
